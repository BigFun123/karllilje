Towards Higher Speed Travel

Karl Lilje, 2018

People often say "It is not possible to travel faster than the speed of light", which is only half true.

The statement can be better phrased as a question - "What is stopping us from traveling faster than the speed of light?"

Long before you reach lightspeed, your spacecraft will be ground to hot dust by particles, the cosmic sandblaster. In space, resistance to particles is defined as mass, so your craft's effective density increases until it can no longer accelerate. The maxima is in fact 50% the speed of light, limited by particle friction and compaction. In theory it is possible to move the particles out the way, creating a "portable wormhole" ahead of the craft. It will require enormous advancements in electromechanical engineering, but there is no reason it can not be done.

Moving particles away ahead of a craft will likely reduce the density in the propellant direction, reducing speed. Modern rockets have a "Rose Pattern" exhaust, which assists the rocket in compressing particles rear of the craft to push the craft forward. So particles need to be channeled outside the surface of the craft where they can form as somethign to push against

Efficient rockets do not have a smooth continuous flame, instead they make a characteristic "pop pop pop" as they create compression disks the rocket pushes against, a type of Jacob's ladder. The rocket is not pushing against the air, it is pushing against the compression disks it creates.

The Time Dilation Myth

Karl Lilje, 2014

Traveling in space doesn't bend time

The myth is propagated by movies like Interstellar, and is based on a misinterpretation of data.

The idea is that a spacecraft traveling away from earth will experience a time distortion, returning in the distant future.

The real Voyager probe sent into out space had on board a Cesium clock. Cesium clocks are remarkably accurate, but not perfect.

A clock does not measure time, it measures the relation of two things. A dial clock measures the rotation of a hand against a notch. A quartz clock measures the number of blips in a piece of quartz.

Cesium is a volatile substance and spontaneously combusts in air. A Cesium clock measures the number of cesium atoms passing through a grate and uses the amount of atoms to calibrate a quartz crystal.

This works reliably on earth, but in space there is a small amount of slippage due to particle density differences, temperature differences, energy, etc.

An area of space with lots of particle motion will have a different effect on cesium decay that the calm of deep space, because particles bumping into atoms changes their emission rate.

So all the Voyager probe proved was the Cesium is not absolutely perfect as a clock source in deep space.

The second misnomer is that satellites experience time distortion. However they do not distort time, their clocks emit at different rates for any number of reasons, including temperature shifts, particle density, or just sample purity.

It's possible the data was simply misunderstood, or the clock manufacturer adjusted perception of the results to maintain its claim of zero loss, which is likely not possible.

To sum up - Time does not bend, buckle, or distort (time is an artifice of the imagination), rather, things behave differently under different conditions.
